## Introduction: Why Multi-Location Management Matters

Running 2-3 restaurant locations is fundamentally different from running one. Without visibility across locations, you're flying blind on critical questions:

- Which location is most efficient?
- Why does Location A's food cost 28% while Location B's is 35%?
- Are we negotiating the best vendor prices at each location?
- Which location's recipe approach should we replicate everywhere?

The cost of not knowing can be **$95K–$185K per year in wasted expenses** — the difference between a thriving multi-location operation and one bleeding money.

This guide shows you how to answer these questions with data, implement cost optimizations, and turn location differences into competitive advantages.

## Getting Started

### Account Setup for Multi-Location Owners

When you create a Kyru account as a multi-location owner:

1. **During onboarding**, specify how many locations you manage (2, 3, 5, etc.)
2. **Add each location** with its name, address, and manager contact
3. **Assign team members** to locations (General Manager per location, staff)
4. **Set up invoicing** — each location records invoices to Kyru (scanning or manual entry)
5. **Enable inventory** — each location takes regular inventory counts

Once set up, the Multi-Location features activate automatically.

### User Roles & Permissions

**Partner/Owner Level**
- Access to all locations' data
- Can view Multi-Location Dashboard, comparisons, best practices
- Can change vendor contracts, make operational decisions
- Full reporting and export access

**General Manager Level**
- Access to their assigned location only
- Cannot see other locations' data (for security/privacy)
- Can see performance metrics for their location
- Can view recommendations but cannot see comparisons

**Staff Level**
- Input invoices, take inventory counts
- No access to metrics or comparisons

### Navigation & Interface

**Multi-Location Dashboard** (main view)
- Shows all locations as cards
- Three tabs: Overview | Recipe Comparison | Vendor Pricing
- Location switcher in top right (drill into individual location)
- "Refresh" button to manually update metrics

**Sidebar Navigation** (to Multi-Location)
- Click "Multi-Location" in main sidebar
- Other sections (Invoices, Recipes, Inventory) focus on single location

## Multi-Location Dashboard: Deep Dive

### Understanding Metrics

The Multi-Location Dashboard displays five core metrics for each location. Here's what they mean and how to interpret them.

#### Food Cost %
**Definition:** (Cost of Goods Sold ÷ Revenue) × 100

**What it shows:** What percentage of your sales goes directly to ingredients.

**Example:** If a location does $100K in sales and spends $28K on food, Food Cost % = 28%.

**Healthy Range:**
- Green: <30% (excellent — industry leaders)
- Yellow: 30-34% (acceptable, room for improvement)
- Red: >34% (action required)

**How to improve:**
- Renegotiate vendor prices (use Vendor Pricing tool)
- Optimize recipes (use Recipe Comparison tool)
- Reduce waste (track inventory accuracy)
- Adjust portion sizes

#### Labor Cost %
**Definition:** (Total Labor Costs ÷ Revenue) × 100

Includes all payroll, taxes, benefits, training for that location.

**Example:** If a location does $100K in sales and payroll is $24K, Labor Cost % = 24%.

**Healthy Range:**
- Green: <25% (excellent)
- Yellow: 25-28% (watch, may need staffing adjustments)
- Red: >28% (likely overstaffed or inefficient scheduling)

**How to improve:**
- Analyze labor productivity by shift/day
- Cross-train staff to reduce redundancy
- Optimize scheduling for customer demand
- Track comp'd meals and give-aways

#### Prime Cost %
**Definition:** ((COGS + Labor Costs) ÷ Revenue) × 100

This is the combined metric showing your two largest expense categories. Industry standard is 55-60%.

**Example:** Food Cost 28% + Labor Cost 24% = Prime Cost 52% (excellent).

**Healthy Range:**
- Green: <55% (excellent — you have room for growth/profit)
- Yellow: 55-60% (industry standard, acceptable)
- Red: >60% (unsustainable — you're losing money or barely breaking even)

**Why Prime Cost matters:**
If your Prime Cost is >60%, you only have <40% of revenue for rent, utilities, supplies, and profit. You cannot sustain this.

#### Inventory Accuracy %
**Definition:** (Actual Inventory Count ÷ System Count) × 100

Measures how well your inventory tracking matches reality.

**Example:** If your system says you have $5,000 of inventory but a physical count shows $4,750, accuracy = 95%.

**Healthy Range:**
- Green: 90%+ (excellent — you trust your data)
- Yellow: 80-89% (significant discrepancies)
- Red: <80% (serious tracking issues)

**Why it matters:**
- Inventory shrink (theft, waste, spoilage) directly hits your food cost
- If accuracy is low, you can't trust your other metrics
- High accuracy = reliable cost projections

**How to improve:**
- Count inventory more frequently (weekly vs. monthly)
- Train staff on proper counting procedures
- Investigate discrepancies (spoilage, expiration, waste)

#### Revenue (Last 30 Days)
**Definition:** Total sales in the past 30 days.

This is context — shows how much business each location is generating. A location with $50K revenue and 30% food cost = $15K food spend. A location with $100K revenue and 30% food cost = $30K food spend. Context matters when making decisions.

### Best Performers Benchmark

At the bottom of the Overview tab, Kyru identifies three leaders:

**Lowest Food Cost**
- Which location has the best food cost percentage
- Shows the gap in dollars: "Location A saves $X/month vs Location B"

**Lowest Labor Cost**
- Which location operates most efficiently with payroll

**Best Prime Cost**
- Which location has the lowest combined food + labor cost (overall efficiency winner)

**How to Use This:**
The best performer is your model. Study what they do differently:
- Which vendors do they use?
- What recipes are they running?
- How are they scheduling labor?
- What is their inventory accuracy?

Then replicate those practices at other locations.

## Recipe Comparison: Deep Dive

### How Recipe Costs Vary Across Locations

A Margarita is a Margarita, right? Not quite. The same recipe costs different amounts to produce at different locations because:

1. **Different vendors** — Location A buys Club Soda from Vendor X, Location B from Vendor Y
2. **Different negotiated rates** — Location B's manager negotiated a better price
3. **Different volumes** — Location A buys in bulk (cheaper per unit), Location B buys smaller quantities (higher unit cost)
4. **Different suppliers for same product** — Location A uses premium limes, Location B uses standard

### The Recipe Comparison Workflow

1. **Open Recipe Comparison tab**
2. **Select a recipe** (dropdown shows recipes available at 2+ locations)
3. **View comparison cards** — one card per location showing:
   - Total recipe cost (e.g., $2.10)
   - Cost % of selling price (e.g., 10.5%)
   - Full ingredient breakdown with unit costs

4. **Identify differences** — which ingredient drives the cost gap?
5. **Take action** — switch vendors or adjust the recipe at the expensive location

### Real Example: Margarita Recipe

**Scenario:** You run 3 locations. Margarita selling price is $20 everywhere.

**Location A (Most Efficient)**
- Total Recipe Cost: $2.10 (10.5% cost)
- Breakdown:
  - Club Soda: 3 oz @ $0.21/oz = $0.63
  - Lime: 0.5 ea @ $0.30 = $0.15
  - Tequila: 1.5 oz @ $0.85/oz = $1.28
  - Ice: included = $0.00

**Location B (Expensive)**
- Total Recipe Cost: $2.45 (12.3% cost)
- Breakdown:
  - Club Soda: 3 oz @ **$0.25/oz** = $0.75 *(+$0.12 vs A)*
  - Lime: 0.5 ea @ **$0.35** = $0.18 *(+$0.03 vs A)*
  - Tequila: 1.5 oz @ **$0.95/oz** = $1.43 *(+$0.15 vs A)*
  - Ice: included = $0.00

**The Gap:** Location A's Margarita is **$0.35 cheaper** per drink.

**Financial Impact:**
- If Location B sells 20 Margaritas/day (conservative estimate)
- Extra cost per day: $0.35 × 20 = $7
- Extra cost per year: $7 × 365 = **$2,555/year**
- With 3 other cocktails at similar gaps: ~**$10K+/year** lost to recipe inefficiency

**Action Items:**
1. Source Club Soda from Location A's vendor for Location B
2. Switch to Location A's lime supplier
3. Negotiate on tequila (or switch to a competitively priced brand)
4. Expected savings: $2,555/year from one recipe alone

### Finding Recipes with Optimization Potential

**Strategy:** Sort by sales volume. Optimize high-volume recipes first.

A $0.35 gap on Margarita saved $2,555/year. Now multiply this across:
- Top 20 recipes by sales volume
- 3 locations
- $0.25–$0.50 gap per recipe (typical range)

**Total opportunity:** $50K–$100K+ annually by optimizing recipes across locations.

## Vendor Pricing Analysis: Deep Dive

### How Vendor Pricing Works Across Locations

Vendors vary by location because:

1. **Different suppliers** — Each location has different local suppliers
2. **Negotiated rates** — A good GM might negotiate better prices
3. **Volume discounts** — Location A buys 500 lbs/month, Location B buys 100 lbs/month
4. **Delivery fees** — Some vendors charge delivery, others don't
5. **Contract terms** — Long-term contracts vs. spot buying

Without visibility, you might have 3 locations paying 3 different prices for the same item.

### The Vendor Pricing Workflow

1. **Open Vendor Pricing tab**
2. **Select an item** (dropdown shows items purchased at 2+ locations, sorted by spend)
3. **View vendor cards** — one per location showing:
   - Vendor name
   - Unit price (e.g., $4.20/lb)
   - Monthly volume
   - Monthly spend
   - Date of last invoice

4. **See consolidation opportunity** — system calculates: "If all locations bought from cheapest vendor, save $X/month"
5. **Take action** — negotiate with current vendors or switch to cheaper option

### Real Example: Chicken Consolidation

**Scenario:** You run 3 locations, each buying chicken monthly.

**Location A** uses Supplier X
- Price: $4.20/lb
- Volume: 200 lbs/month
- Monthly spend: $840

**Location B** uses Supplier Y
- Price: $3.85/lb *(best price)*
- Volume: 200 lbs/month
- Monthly spend: $770

**Location C** uses Supplier X
- Price: $4.10/lb
- Volume: 200 lbs/month
- Monthly spend: $820

**Current Total:** $2,430/month

**If All Used Supplier Y:**
- 600 lbs/month @ $3.85/lb = $2,310/month
- **Monthly savings: $120**
- **Annual savings: $1,440**

But wait — that's just chicken. Now repeat this for:
- Beef (savings: $1,200)
- Pork (savings: $800)
- Produce (savings: $2,500)
- Beverages (savings: $1,800)
- Dairy (savings: $900)
- Dry goods (savings: $1,250)

**Total annual consolidation savings: $9,490** — on a handful of categories. With 20+ SKUs, easily $50K+/year.

### Unit Normalization

The system handles unit conversion automatically. For example:
- Location A buys chicken in **lbs**
- Location B buys chicken in **oz**
- Location C buys chicken in **lbs**

The system normalizes to a common unit (pounds) so you can compare apples-to-apples. It shows: "6 oz bottles converted to lb equivalent for comparison."

## Best Practices: 8-10 Operational Strategies

### 1. Weekly Metric Review

**What:** Every Monday morning, spend 15 minutes reviewing the Multi-Location Dashboard.

**Why:** Catch red flags early. Yellow metrics can become red in 2-3 weeks if not addressed.

**How:**
- Open Multi-Location Dashboard
- Note any red metrics (Food Cost >34%, Labor >28%, Prime >60%)
- Check which location needs attention
- Send note to that location's manager: "Your food cost is 35% this week. Let's discuss vendor pricing."

### 2. Monthly Cost Optimization Meetings

**What:** Once a month, gather GMs + owner to review comparisons.

**Why:** Implement changes together. GMs understand local constraints (maybe Location B's neighborhood has limited suppliers).

**Who:** Owner + all General Managers (in person or video call)

**Agenda (30 minutes):**
- Which recipe has the biggest cost gap? Can we replicate best practice?
- Which vendor offers consolidation savings? Who wants to switch?
- Inventory accuracy — how are we doing?
- Labor efficiency — any staffing changes needed?

### 3. Vendor Negotiation Strategy

**Leverage your data:**

- You: "Location B pays $3.85/lb for chicken. Can you match that here?"
- Vendor: "That's our best rate. Can't go lower."
- You: "Then we're consolidating all 3 locations to that vendor."

**Results:** Suppliers get more volume, you get better prices.

### 4. Team Alignment & Knowledge Sharing

**What:** When Location A finds a winning recipe or vendor, document it and roll it out to other locations.

**Example:** Location A negotiates Tequila @ $0.85/oz (best price). Share this vendor contact with Locations B & C.

**How:**
- Recipe Comparison shows Location A's approach
- Share vendor contact + price
- New locations adopt it
- Savings compound

### 5. Performance Benchmarking

**Track improvement over time:**

- Month 1: Location B food cost = 35% (red)
- Month 2: Location B food cost = 33% (yellow) — good progress
- Month 3: Location B food cost = 30% (green) — success!

Review trends quarterly. Don't judge location managers on one bad week; judge on 3-month trajectories.

### 6. Avoid Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1:** Ignoring yellow metrics.
- Yellow (30-34% food cost) is a warning sign.
- Address now before it becomes red (>34%).

**Mistake 2:** Not implementing recipe optimizations.
- Data shows Margarita costs $0.35 more at Location B
- But manager doesn't switch vendors, so waste continues
- **$2,555/year lost to inaction**

**Mistake 3:** Comparing locations unfairly.
- Location A is downtown (high rent, premium customers, higher prices)
- Location B is suburban (lower rent, budget-conscious customers, lower prices)
- Their food cost %s might be similar even if absolute dollars differ
- Context matters

### 7. Seasonal Cost Adjustments

**Fact:** Food costs vary seasonally.
- Produce is cheaper in season (summer) vs. off-season (winter)
- Holiday items skew costs during peak seasons
- Labor demand varies (busy seasons = higher labor %)

**Strategy:**
- Track metrics monthly, not daily
- Expect seasonal variations — don't panic
- Compare month-to-month YoY (May 2025 vs. May 2026) for fair comparison

### 8. New Location Onboarding

**When opening a new location:**

1. Set up invoicing from day 1
2. Start taking inventory weekly (not monthly) initially
3. Use best practices from successful locations (recipes, vendors)
4. First 90 days: expect metrics to fluctuate as you find local suppliers
5. By month 4-6: Location should stabilize with benchmarks

### 9. Role of the General Manager

**GMs are your operational leverage:**

- Each GM controls recipes, staffing, vendor relationships
- GM's performance directly impacts metrics
- Good GMs = low food cost, efficient labor
- Poor GMs = high costs, high waste

**Incentivize performance:**
- Tie bonuses to Prime Cost reduction
- Reward GMs who find vendor savings (e.g., split the savings: 70% to location, 30% to GM)
- Recognize best performers publicly

### 10. Communication of Results

**Share wins across the organization:**

- "Location A reduced food cost from 32% to 28% by switching vendors. Here's how..."
- "This month's best practice: Location C's prep process reduced waste by 3%. Let's document and teach everyone."
- "We consolidated chicken purchases. Saving $1,440/year."

## Advanced Topics

### Seasonal Cost Variations

Food costs follow patterns:

**Summer (High Season)**
- Fresh produce is abundant = lower cost
- Lighter dishes (salads, grilled items) are popular = lower food cost %
- Tourist traffic = higher revenue = lower % costs
- Ice cream, beverages spike = higher COGS
- A/C costs spike = higher utilities (not food cost, but labor/overhead)

**Fall (Transition)**
- Harvest foods are cheap
- Fall specials (pumpkin, apple items) are popular
- Begin transitioning to comfort foods

**Winter (Low Season)**
- Produce is expensive (imported, out of season)
- Comfort foods are popular (higher-cost ingredients)
- Holidays spike (expensive ingredients for special dishes)
- Tourist traffic is low = lower revenue = higher % costs (fixed costs spread over lower sales)

**Spring (Transition)**
- Prices stabilize as spring produce arrives
- Easter/Mother's Day create demand spikes
- Weather improves = outdoor activities = lower restaurant traffic

**Strategy:** Compare March 2025 vs. March 2026, not March vs. April. Seasonal comparisons are misleading.

### Multi-Year Trend Analysis

**One-month data is noise. Six-month data is trend. One-year data is reality.**

Example:
- January: Food cost 32% (winter, high-cost ingredients)
- February: Food cost 31%
- March: Food cost 30%
- April: Food cost 29%
- May: Food cost 28%
- June: Food cost 28%

**Trend:** Food cost is declining from 32% → 28% over 6 months. This is real improvement, not seasonal variation.

**How to identify:**
- Plot monthly food cost %s over 12 months
- Draw a trend line
- If trend is down and to the right, you're improving (good!)
- If trend is up, you're degrading (fix it!)

## Troubleshooting & FAQ

**Q: Why does my recipe comparison show "Recipe not offered" at one location?**
A: That recipe hasn't been created at that location yet. Only recipes created at 2+ locations show comparisons. Create the recipe at the missing location, then it will appear.

**Q: My vendor prices look wrong. The system shows $4.20/lb but I think I negotiated $3.95/lb.**
A: The system pulls the most recent invoice. If you just negotiated a new rate but haven't received an invoice at that price, the old price still shows. Once an invoice comes through at the new price, the system updates.

**Q: Location B's food cost suddenly spiked from 30% to 38%. What happened?**
A: Investigate:
1. Did an invoice come through at a higher price?
2. Did they change a supplier?
3. Did they stop counting waste/spoilage?
4. Did they reduce revenue (less sales, same food costs = higher %)?
5. Was there a holiday menu with premium ingredients?

Contact the GM and audit. Spikes usually indicate a specific issue, not gradual drift.

**Q: Can I compare just 2 locations instead of all 3?**
A: Yes. The system shows all locations by default, but you can focus. Click on the comparison cards for the 2 locations you're interested in and ignore the others.

**Q: My inventory accuracy is terrible (60%). What should I do?**
A: 
1. Identify sources of shrink (theft, waste, expiration, spoilage)
2. Count inventory more frequently (weekly vs. monthly)
3. Train staff on proper counting
4. Investigate discrepancies larger than $200
5. Expect 3-6 months to improve to 85%+

**Q: How do I know if my color thresholds are right for my business?**
A: The thresholds (Green <30% food, <25% labor, <55% prime) are industry standard. But your specific situation matters:
- Fine dining restaurants typically run 32-35% food cost
- Quick-service runs 25-28%
- Casual dining runs 28-32%

If your entire business is consistently above thresholds, ask: is this my business model, or am I inefficient? Check against competitors in your segment.

## Glossary of Terms

**Prime Cost:** Food Cost % + Labor Cost %. The two biggest expense categories.

**Inventory Accuracy:** How closely your system count matches physical count. Higher is better.

**Consolidation:** Combining purchases of the same item from multiple locations to a single vendor for better rates.

**Food Cost %:** (COGS ÷ Revenue) × 100. What percent of sales goes to ingredients.

**COGS:** Cost of Goods Sold. Direct ingredient costs for recipes and menu items.

**Vendor:** Supplier of ingredients (e.g., Sysco, US Foods, local produce supplier).

**SKU:** Stock Keeping Unit. Individual item in your inventory (e.g., "Club Soda, 2-liter bottle").
